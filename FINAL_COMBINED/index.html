Chemical Components Translation

  • Alcohol: Alcohol by volume (the good stuff)
  • pH: Overall measure of acidity
  • Citric Acid: What typically makes something sour, adds freshness
  • Residual Sugar: What sugar is left after fermentation, controls sweetness of the wine
  • Sulphates: Prevents oxidation in the wine, keeps it fresh throughout storage time
  • TSD: What SO2 is added overall, both free to fight oxidation and bonded to other chemicals in the wine
  • FSD: What SO2 remains free in the wine to keep it fresh and fight oxidation
  • Density: Translates into the strength of the wine and its flavors
  • Chlorides: The amount of salt in the wine
  • Vol. Acidity: The amount of acetic acid, can lead to unpleasant vinegar taste
  • Fixed Acidity: All acids in the wine, ones that don’t evaporate

Takeaways

For white wines, the wines have larger variation between high quality wines (7-8) and low quality wines (3-4). For high quality wines, they tend to have higher alcohol content, which goes well with white wines higher levels of sweetness overall. High quality white wines also have low density, most likely due to the thought that white wine is supposed to be a lighter wine than red. For the low quality wines, high acidity and sulfur dioxides are common. This is because both make the wines more bitter tasting, something white wine drinkers often avoid.

For red wines, there is little differentiation in high and low quality wine in terms of their chemical attributes. Both the highest and lowest rankings, eight and three respectively, have higher density, alcohol levels, and pH. The difference comes from the highest quality having lower sugar and less sulfates than the lowest quality. However, there is not much differentiation at all when it comes to chemical attributes , so just pick whichever one has the prettiest bottle (that's what I do)
